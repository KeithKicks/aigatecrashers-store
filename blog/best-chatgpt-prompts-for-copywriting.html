Best ChatGPT Prompts for Copywriting in 2026: 10 Prompts That Sell

AI Gatecrashers Team · April 20, 2026

Copywriting is the highest-leverage skill in business. The right words on a sales page can double revenue. The wrong words on an ad can burn your entire budget in an afternoon. ChatGPT doesn't replace the strategic thinking behind great copy — but it eliminates the blank page problem, generates variations at speed, and helps you think through persuasion frameworks that professional copywriters charge thousands to apply.

This guide gives you 10 specific, tested ChatGPT prompts for the most common copywriting tasks. Each prompt includes the full text you can copy and paste, an explanation of why it works, and tips for customizing it to your business.

Why Prompt Structure Matters for Copywriting

Before we get to the prompts, understand this: the quality of AI-generated copy is almost entirely a function of prompt quality. Generic prompts produce generic copy. Specific prompts produce copy that sounds like it was written by someone who understands your audience, your product, and your market.

Every good copywriting prompt includes four elements: context (who you are, what you sell), audience (who you're writing for and what they care about), format (the specific deliverable — headline, email, ad, etc.), and constraints (tone, length, style, things to avoid). Miss any one of these and the output suffers.

Prompt 1: Headline Generator

"You are a direct-response copywriter. Write 15 headline variations for a [product type] targeting [audience]. The main benefit is [benefit]. Use a mix of: curiosity headlines, benefit-driven headlines, how-to headlines, and fear-of-missing-out headlines. Each should be under 12 words. Avoid cliches like 'unlock,' 'game-changer,' and 'revolutionize.'"

This prompt works because it specifies the role (direct-response copywriter, not generic assistant), the quantity (15 variations gives you plenty to choose from), the variety (four headline types), the constraint (12 words), and the exclusions (cliches that make copy feel AI-generated). The exclusion list is especially important — it's what makes the output sound human.

Prompt 2: Sales Page Structure

"Write a complete sales page outline for [product name], a [product description] priced at [price] for [target audience]. Follow this structure: attention-grabbing headline, problem agitation (3 specific pain points), solution introduction, 5 key benefits with supporting details, social proof section (suggest what types of proof to include), offer stack listing everything included, price justification, FAQ section (5 common objections answered), and a final CTA with urgency element. Write the full copy for each section. Tone: [describe tone]. Total length: 1,500-2,000 words."

This is a power prompt. It gives ChatGPT the entire sales page framework — the classic direct-response structure that has been selling products for decades. The output isn't a finished sales page, but it's a complete first draft that captures 80% of the strategic heavy lifting. Your job becomes editing and refining rather than creating from scratch.

Prompt 3: Facebook/Meta Ad Copy

"Write 5 Facebook ad copy variations for [product/service]. Target audience: [describe]. Primary goal: [clicks/conversions/awareness]. Each ad should include: a hook in the first line that stops the scroll, 2-3 sentences of body copy addressing the main pain point, a clear CTA. Keep each variation under 125 words. Variation styles: 1) story-based, 2) question-based, 3) statistic-based, 4) testimonial-style, 5) direct benefit. No emojis in the first two variations."

Ad copy needs to be punchy, specific, and varied for testing. This prompt forces five different angles on the same product, which gives you a real A/B testing slate. The "no emojis in the first two" constraint is deliberate — it gives you both emoji-heavy and clean variations to test what performs better with your audience.

Prompt 4: Product Descriptions

"Write a product description for [product name]. It's a [what it is] that helps [audience] to [outcome]. Key features: [list 3-5 features]. Write two versions: Version A should be benefit-focused and emotional (150 words), leading with the transformation the customer experiences. Version B should be feature-focused and practical (150 words), leading with specifications and what's included. Both should end with a one-line CTA."

Two versions of the same product description let you test emotional vs. rational approaches. E-commerce products often do better with benefit-focused copy, while B2B and technical products often perform better with feature-focused copy. This prompt gives you both in one shot.

Prompt 5: Email Subject Lines That Get Opens

"Generate 20 email subject lines for a [campaign type] email about [topic] to [audience]. Organize them into four categories: 1) Curiosity-gap lines that make the reader need to know the answer, 2) Benefit-first lines that lead with value, 3) Social proof lines that imply popularity or results, 4) Personal/conversational lines that feel like they're from a friend. Each under 50 characters. No ALL CAPS. No excessive punctuation."

Twenty subject lines organized by strategy gives you a testing framework, not just a list. You can test across categories to learn which emotional trigger works best for your list, then double down on the winning category.

Prompt 6: Landing Page Hero Section

"Write 3 variations of a landing page hero section for [product/service]. Each variation should include: a main headline (under 10 words), a subheadline that expands on the promise (under 25 words), 3 bullet points highlighting key benefits, and CTA button text. Variation 1: lead with the pain point. Variation 2: lead with the aspiration. Variation 3: lead with social proof or a number. Target audience: [describe]."

The hero section is the highest-impact real estate on any landing page. Three variations with different lead angles gives you a meaningful split test that can move your conversion rate by 20-50%.

Prompt 7: Value Proposition Statement

"Help me write a value proposition for [company/product]. We help [target customer] to [primary outcome] by [how we do it differently]. Write 5 versions: 1) One-sentence version (under 15 words), 2) Elevator pitch version (50 words), 3) Website hero version with headline + subheadline, 4) Email introduction version (100 words), 5) Detailed positioning statement (150 words). Each should emphasize what makes us different from [competitors/alternatives]."

Most businesses can't articulate their value proposition clearly because they've never been forced to do it at multiple lengths. This prompt generates five formats from one session, and the shortest ones force the kind of brutal clarity that makes all your copy better.

Prompt 8: Testimonial Request Email

"Write a testimonial request email to send to customers of [product/service]. The email should: thank them genuinely (not generically), explain why testimonials matter to the business, make it easy by providing 3 specific questions they can answer (about their problem before, their experience with the product, and their results after), and offer to write a draft based on their answers if they prefer. Tone: grateful and low-pressure. Under 200 words."

Social proof is the foundation of persuasive copy, and most businesses don't have enough of it because they never ask. This prompt creates a testimonial request email that actually gets responses because it reduces friction — the three specific questions give the customer a framework instead of asking them to write from a blank page.

Prompt 9: Objection-Handling Copy Blocks

"List the top 7 objections someone might have before buying [product] at [price point]. For each objection, write a 50-75 word copy block that acknowledges the concern, reframes it, and resolves it with evidence or logic. These will be used in a sales page FAQ section. Tone: empathetic but confident. Avoid being defensive."

FAQ sections on sales pages aren't really about answering questions — they're about handling objections in disguise. This prompt makes that strategy explicit, producing copy that addresses buying resistance head-on.

Prompt 10: Brand Voice Copy Calibration

"Here's a sample of my brand's writing: [paste 200-300 words of your existing copy]. Analyze this sample and identify: the tone (formal/casual/somewhere between), the sentence structure patterns, the vocabulary level, any recurring phrases or patterns, and the overall personality. Then rewrite the following [type of copy] in this same voice: [describe what you need written]."

This is a meta-prompt — it teaches ChatGPT your voice before asking it to write. The output will sound like your brand instead of like ChatGPT, which is the single biggest quality improvement you can make in AI-assisted copywriting. Once you've calibrated, save the voice analysis and paste it at the start of every future copywriting session.

Making AI Copy Sound Human

The best AI-generated copy doesn't sound like AI. Here's how to ensure your outputs pass the human test:

Add imperfection deliberately. Real writing has personality — sentence fragments, rhetorical questions, the occasional short paragraph that's just one word. Edit your ChatGPT drafts to include these human touches.

Replace generic examples with specific ones. ChatGPT might say "many businesses have seen success." You should change that to "Sarah's Etsy shop went from 12 to 340 sales in three months." Specificity is the hallmark of persuasive copy.

Cut the filler. ChatGPT tends to over-explain. Go through the output and cut every sentence that doesn't add new information or move the reader toward the CTA. Tighter copy converts better.
